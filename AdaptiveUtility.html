Welcome to the Economics Experiment!

Below, you will find a 'game' where you get to strategically set your preferences for different point values. To play, simply drag each of the point values into one of the four ranking tiers (first choice is favorite, fourth is least favorite).

Once you have ranked all the values, click the "Done Ranking" button. The game will randomly select two of the values and give you whichever of the two you ranked as better. If you ranked both the same, one will be randomly selected, which might end up being the lower option, resulting in a missed opportunity. You can then change your rankings and draw another two values. Watch out, though, because not all the of the point values have the same odds of being selected by the computer.

Now let's be real, the game is a little tedious and takes some time before you can learn how to optimize your strategy, which is why I paid people to play by converting their scores into real money. The results of this economics experiment can be found here: Adaptive Utility -- Digital Commons.

By incentivizing players based on their score we can start to see how they adapt their preferences over time. Specifically, we want to know what it takes for people to start changing their initial strategies and how well they can identify the correct rankings based on a sampling distribution.

Optimal Play

The core winning strategy of the game follows two general rules:

  1. Rank all values in order so that no small value is ranked higher than a large value
  2. Avoid ties as much as possible while following (1)

The first rule is pretty trivial and almost all players follow it immediately; there are no reasons for us to ever want to intentionally recieve a lower payment. The second is harder because no matter how you organize your preferences it is still possible for a tie to occur. Moreover, the player is not told which values are more likely to be selected. If you played the game, or if you just click the "Done Ranking" button a few times, you will likely observe that the larger point values get selected with much higher frequency.

Since ties occur when two values are ranked the same, the goal then should be to spread out frequently selected values between different ranking tiers. We don't want to go too far, though, because a tie between 60 and 610 could result in a much greater loss than a tie between 60 and 110, for example.

Finding the optimal strategy with incomplete knowledge

The above ranking is extremely slanted to one side; it is very unlikely for a person to just stumble upon this to start. Instead, players must find a winning strategy via trial and error by collecting information over the course of each round.

Let us first imagine if we were to program a computer play this game. One option would be to have it estimate a probability distribution based on past results in each round, then calculate all possible outcomes based on that distribution. It could then define an entirely new strategy each round as needed. Another possible method is to employ some kind of machine-learning algorithm that updates its rankings whenever it makes a mistake. This algorithm might focus on just the cut-off points and shift one or two values up or down as it learns.

Both of the above options can eventually converge to the optimal strategy, but neither describe exactly how a human can play the game. Most of us cannot calculate probability distributions in our head, nor do we tend to follow exact algorithms. Moreover, we may consider attributes of the game other than long term average earnings.

How do people play?

When I ran this expirement on 65 subjects, I found that three broad categories of player types existed, but with lots of variation within each category.

  1. Players who identify a strategy early and make changes slowly (~54%)
  2. Players who changed their strategies frequently round to round (~37%)
  3. Players who couldn't figure out rule (1) in their rankings by the end of the experiment (~9%)

Both type 1 and 2 players were largely successful at the game, though no players ever reached the fully-optimal ranking shown above. Both types were most likely to change their rankings immediately after a round where they lost some points, but type 1 players appear to also be influenced by the general probabilities being revealed, even when they recieved the higher value. Type 2 players were more likely to make large changes to their rankings, whereas type 1 players tend to shift just one or two at a time. Type 1 players' willingness to adapt based on information allow them to slightly outperform type 2 players in later rounds. In a longer game, type 2 players trial and error approach might achieve the same results.

Overall, these play patterns suggest a learning process which is both cognitive and reactive: players consider probabilities and general strategic principals, but also respond most readily when presented with an immediate loss. I also noted that many players presented significant aversion to the lowest payoffs. Even after identifying correct strategies elsewhere, they would isolate the smallest payoffs in their rankings to avoid them. This likely occurs due to the payment structure of the experiment: people want to ensure they make at least a certain amount of money for their time.

In this average ranking we observe a clustering of rankings in the third choice bin, rather than fourth choice. This could occur due to aversion to the lowest payoffs (people really want to avoid the small values). Alternatively, those values occur with the lowest frequency, so players might just need more rounds to adjust their strategies in the lower bins where they don't recieve frequent information.

Notably, I also ran a second trial with the probabilities reversed so that the small payoffs occurred with more frequency. Under this treatment players were much quicker to adjust their rankings, usually doing so within 1-2 rounds. Reactive (type 2) players were actually quicker to improve their expected returns relative to type 1 players due to their immediate reaction to recieving one of the lowest two payoffs. Similar to the first treatment, players did not reach optimal rankings for the infrequently visited portion of the distribution, suggesting that players either don't care about the less common outcomes our just needed more time to fine-tune those rankings.

Conclusion

Thank you for reading this far! This article serves as just a brief summary of some of the ideas captured in the broader research I conducted during my thesis. If you want to read more of the background information or see any of the proofs, please follow: Adaptive Utility -- Digital Commons. Overall, this project provides some fun insights into the way people process events. We clearly observe both the learning process and some inherent biases in how we prioritize new information in the timing and frequency of how people change their strategies.
